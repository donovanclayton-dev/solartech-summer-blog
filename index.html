AI Is Driving Up Your California Electric Bill. Here's How to Protect Yourself.

California's electric bills have been rising for years. But a new pressure is building that most homeowners haven't heard about yet — and the numbers suggest it won't be quiet for long. AI-driven data centers are consuming electricity at a pace that is reshaping the state's grid, and the cost of keeping up with that demand is likely to fall on ordinary households.

The AI Energy Boom — By the Numbers

In 2023, data centers consumed roughly 4.4% of all U.S. electricity. By 2028, Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory projects that figure will climb to somewhere between 6.7% and 12% of total national electricity consumption. The primary driver is artificial intelligence — the massive computing clusters that power AI models require extraordinary amounts of power to run and cool, around the clock.

California is not sitting this out. The state already ranks third nationally in data center capacity, and the California Energy Commission forecasts an additional 4 to 6 gigawatts of new data center demand by 2035. To put that in perspective, 1 gigawatt can power roughly 750,000 average California homes.

"A January 2026 report predicts that U.S. data centers' total energy demand will nearly double between 2025 and 2028 — the equivalent of adding a country with the energy needs of Spain in just three years."

What's Already Happening to Your Bill

You don't have to wait until 2028 to feel it. Residential electricity prices rose 11% from January to August 2025 — nearly four times the rate of overall inflation. Utility rate requests and approvals totaled $34 billion in the first three quarters of 2025, more than double the $16 billion sought in the same period the year prior.

Southern California Edison customers are already absorbing the latest round of increases. The CPUC approved a 10% rate hike for SCE effective October 2025, adding an average of $17 per month — or $200 per year — to household bills. A typical customer using 500 kWh per month saw their bill jump from $171 to over $193.

California's Specific Exposure

The data center buildout is creating direct infrastructure costs that, without strong policy protections, flow straight to ratepayers. California's grid operator CAISO has already approved a cluster of transmission upgrades in the South Bay Area — largely to serve 2.5 gigawatts of concentrated data center load growth projected between 2026 and 2039. That single cluster of upgrades costs over $2 billion, and it's just one piece of the total picture.

Other states offer a preview of where this goes. In the PJM electricity market — stretching from Illinois to North Carolina — data centers drove an estimated $9.3 billion price increase in the 2025–26 capacity market alone. The result: average residential bills expected to rise by $18 per month in parts of Maryland and $16 per month in Ohio. In the Mid-Atlantic region overall, the grid operator paid $14.7 billion for power in the 2025/26 delivery year — compared to $2.2 billion the year before.

What Policy Makers Are — and Aren't — Doing

The legislative response in California has been largely blocked. Attempts to shield regular Californians from footing the bill for data center infrastructure in 2025 ended with a law that requires regulators to write a report about the issue — due in 2027. That means findings won't be ready in time for lawmakers to act in 2026, leaving a significant policy window unaddressed.

Governor Newsom demonstrated clear support for the data center industry in 2025, vetoing legislation that would have regulated water and electricity usage while streamlining regulatory review for renewable energy data center projects. Big Tech lobbied hard against tighter rules. One observer noted that energy bills, even when large, are essentially "rounding errors" for these companies — which raises the question of why they'd resist paying a bit more to ensure costs aren't shifted onto other customers.

On a more encouraging note, California's Little Hoover Commission released a report in March 2026 recommending that large data centers should pay the full cost of the infrastructure and grid services they require — including prepayment for grid infrastructure and contributions toward wildfire safety costs. Whether Sacramento acts on those recommendations remains to be seen.

The Honest Picture

The debate isn't entirely one-sided. PG&E has argued that every gigawatt of new data center load can lower average bills by 1% or more, because fixed grid costs get spread across more customers. A 2025 analysis by Energy and Environmental Economics reached a similar conclusion for large-load customers who pay more than the minimum cost to serve them.

But as Mark Toney of the Utility Reform Network put it: "Data center growth has as much potential to increase electricity rates as it does to decrease rates — if not done properly." Without the protections that Big Tech successfully lobbied away in 2025, California households are essentially gambling on the goodwill of the industry. A Carnegie Mellon University study estimates that data centers and cryptocurrency mining could lead to an 8% increase in the average U.S. electricity bill by 2030 — and potentially exceeding 25% in the highest-demand markets.
